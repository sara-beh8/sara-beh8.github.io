🧲 Demand generation
My job was to find the company's next productUsercentrics — Incubation team · 2025

The problem

Usercentrics' incubation team had a mandate: validate at least five new product concepts a year — with real market demand, before any engineering spend.

What I built

A repeatable demand-test engine: framework-driven market research → ICP definition → landing pages → paid campaigns on LinkedIn, Google Ads, and community channels (Reddit, Quora). Measured like an investor, not a media buyer: CAC, CLV, CPL, lead volume, plus qualitative feedback on product needs. I defined the validation criterion itself: a concept passed when CAC fell over the test window — falling acquisition cost means compounding demand, not just spend.

The result

  • 3 products validated · 5 ideas killed · 1 pivoted — the kills saved months of build cost
  • Best test drove cost per lead to €14 within one month
  • One validated concept — privacy-friendly analytics — was pushed forward to build

What it taught me

Killing an idea with clean evidence is as valuable as validating one — and much cheaper than finding out after launch.

The Trustpilot hack that changed my careerUnbabel — BDR → Growth Engineer · 2022

The problem

As a BDR selling translation-AI, I was manually prospecting — slow, and blind to which companies actually needed us.

What I built

I learned the Python I needed in a week and built a tool that scraped Trustpilot reviews of enterprise prospects and checked one thing: did the company reply in the language the customer complained in? A mismatch meant a company visibly failing at multilingual support — a perfectly qualified prospect. I layered data enrichment on top to see what tools they used.

The result

  • Cold pitches became consultative calls: "here's what your customers are actually experiencing"
  • Promoted to Growth Engineer on the back of the tool

What it taught me

The habit that's defined every role since: if a process bores you, that's the signal to automate it.

🔁 Lifecycle & retention
A lifecycle program where old campaigns create new onesGorgias — Lifecycle Growth Manager · 2025–26

The problem

I inherited ~15 lifecycle campaigns running in parallel at an AI customer-experience platform serving 15,000+ ecommerce brands — some campaigns over a year old, carrying outdated product info, stale designs, and naming conventions from three generations of previous owners. Overlapping audiences, no documentation home, no renewal process. Built, launched, forgotten.

What I built

An operating system that renews itself: AI-assisted audits of every campaign → rebuilds that must pull current product context → governance rails (mandatory eligibility baseline, exclusion lists, standardized naming + UTM conventions) → a campaign registry logging every renewal → the whole build pipeline (design → segment → copy → HTML → QA) codified as Claude Code skills, with prompts versioned and evaluated.

The result

  • Old campaigns now systematically generate their successors (Weekly Email v1→v2→v3; winback v1→v2)
  • Team closed Q1 2026 at 106% of net-new ARR plan; AI cross-sell at 167%
  • An A/B test shipping every ~2 weeks across 10–15 campaigns
  • The system outlived me — handed over as documentation + skills, not tribal knowledge

What it taught me

A campaign is an artifact; a renewal loop is an asset. Build the loop.

Making the metrics honest before optimizing themGorgias — Weekly Email rebuild · 2026

The problem

A weekly newsletter to ~14k accounts looked healthy at 44.8% opens. It wasn't: flows were broken, ~50% of the cohort silently never received emails, segmentation was one-size-fits-all, and the "44.8%" was inflated by Apple's machine opens.

What I did

First, honesty: I separated human from machine metrics — real human opens were 22.7% — and reset the campaign's success criteria on human numbers. Then the rebuild: engagement-bucket segmentation (Active / Ignoring / Dark, each with its own flow), a two-step AI personalization pipeline with hard guardrails against hallucinated URLs and product names, proper exclusion logic, and a metrics migration to ClickHouse — working alongside the data intelligence team.

The result

  • Personalization test won with +5.6% CTR; follow-up test lifted CTOR from 6.7% toward a ≥10% target
  • Segmentation projected +24% opens & clicks on 17% less volume
  • And one honest failure: the first AI-tips variant failed QA (10.5% render errors, hallucinated links). I held it back, documented the negative result, and shipped the guardrail system instead. That guardrail system is now the foundation the pipeline runs on.

What it taught me

Optimizing an inflated metric is decorating a broken house. Fix the measurement first — even when the honest number looks worse.

🤖 Systems & AI
Automating a sales team's research jobGorgias — outbound automation · 2026

The problem

Booking sales calls required manual account research — the classic SDR grind: find the right accounts, understand their situation, write a relevant email. Slow, inconsistent, unscalable.

What I built

The whole motion, end-to-end: account research on defined data traits in BigQuery → Claude-personalized email sequences → orchestrated through n8n → delivered via Customer.io, with separate variants for self-serve (PLG) and sales-led (SLG) accounts. I also co-built the next iteration with the data intelligence team: a "context layer" where an LLM diagnoses 10,000+ accounts against product-usage signals and takes automated actions (Claude + Cargo + LangSmith, prompts versioned in GitHub).

The result

  • Booked sales calls grew from ≈20–30 to ≈50–55 per week
  • Context-layer pilot: +5% automated interactions in its first cohort

What it taught me

AI doesn't replace the growth playbook — it removes the excuse for not running it on every account.
